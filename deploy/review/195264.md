## Test Strategies

### Strategy 1: Undef/poison constant complement lanes

- **Target**: Mutate scalar and vector tests from a normal `(A ^ (X & ~C)) ^ C` case to use `undef`, `poison`, and mixed vector constants such as `<i8 240, i8 poison, i8 undef, i8 0>` paired with their `not` constants.
- **Rationale**: The fold checks `NotC == ConstantExpr::getNot(C1)` on `m_ImmConstant` operands. `m_ImmConstant` can bind constants containing undef/poison, and bitwise transforms involving such constants require careful refinement reasoning because different uses of undef may choose different values while poison propagates. Existing neighboring folds often use `replaceUndefsWith`/`mergeUndefsWith`, but this new fold does not.
- **Expected Issue**: If the matcher treats poison/undef constants as ordinary complements in a way that is not a valid refinement, the optimized form may become more defined or differently poison than the source. Even if correct, this guards against future regressions in constant matching and ConstantExpr::getNot handling.

### Strategy 2: Vector splat and mixed-lane complement matching

- **Target**: Mutate existing xor/bitwise vector tests to fixed vectors and scalable-vector splats: `(<4 x i32> %a ^ (%x & <...~C...>)) ^ <...C...>`, including both exact full vectors and splat constants.
- **Rationale**: `m_ImmConstant` accepts vector constants and has splat-specific behavior. The proof is scalar bitwise algebra, but InstCombine applies it elementwise to vectors. Constant uniquing/equality for vector constants with poison/undef lanes is a common edge case.
- **Expected Issue**: The fold may fail to fire for semantically splatted vector constants, or may mishandle poison lanes in splats, because `m_ImmConstant` has special splat handling while the equality test compares the full `Constant *` returned by `ConstantExpr::getNot(C1)`. A missed fold or incorrect lane result would be visible in FileCheck output.

### Strategy 3: Constant A canonicalization-loop stress

- **Target**: Mutate scalar tests so `%A` is replaced by constants including `0`, all-ones, equal to `C`, and unrelated constants; run instcombine with FileCheck for stable output.
- **Rationale**: The new fold returns `(X | C) ^ A` without excluding `A` as an immediate constant. Earlier in `visitXor`, there is a fold for `(X | C2) ^ C1 --> (X & ~C2) ^ (C1^C2)`. If `A` is constant, the newly created instruction can immediately match that earlier canonicalization. The result may be stable, but it should be tested for no loop and expected canonical form.
- **Expected Issue**: A transformation cycle or poor canonicalization may occur when `A` is itself a constant: the new result `(X | C) ^ A` matches earlier xor-with-constant folds that canonicalize `(X | C2) ^ C1` back toward an `and` plus xor constant. This could cause repeated combining or unexpected final form.

### Strategy 4: One-use and commuted-shape coverage

- **Target**: Mutate tests to all operand orderings: outer constant on lhs/rhs, inner `A` on lhs/rhs, and `and` operands swapped. Add negative variants where the inner xor or and has an extra use.
- **Rationale**: The matcher nests `m_OneUse(m_c_Xor(... m_OneUse(m_c_And(...))))` inside a commutative outer xor and also uses a commutative inner xor/and. This creates several structural variants. The fold relies on one-use restrictions for the inner xor and the and, but not on `A` or `X`. Tests should ensure only the intended DAG shape folds and equivalent operand orderings are covered.
- **Expected Issue**: If one-use constraints are accidentally too weak under commuted matching, InstCombine could duplicate an expression with other uses or change profitability. If too strong, valid variants may be missed. The expected observable issue is either unexpected fold despite extra uses or missed fold for a permitted commuted one-use shape.

### Strategy 5: Non-power-of-two APInt widths

- **Target**: Mutate scalar tests from `i32` to `i1`, `i7`, `i37`, and a large APInt type, selecting constants whose complements are nontrivial in that width.
- **Rationale**: The fold is bitwise and should be width-parametric. `ConstantExpr::getNot(C1)` and printed negative constants can be subtle for non-standard widths such as i1, i7, i37, or i257. Testing these protects against hidden assumptions in constant complementing and canonicalization.
- **Expected Issue**: For unusual integer widths, an incorrect assumption about all-ones masks or constant complement construction would show up as wrong constants in the optimized IR. This is especially useful for non-byte and large APInt widths.

### Analysis

No LLVM InstCombine correctness bug was found for this patch.

Bug Triggering Analysis: The reviewed fold is `(A ^ (X & ~C)) ^ C -> (X | C) ^ A`. I tested representative positive, negative, and edge-case inputs derived from all managed tests. Alive2 proved the relevant transformations correct for scalar i32, non-power-of-two integer widths (i1/i7/i37/i257 where supported), vector splats, non-splat vectors, commuted outer/inner/and operand shapes, and poison-lane vector constants. The extra-use tests confirmed that the `m_OneUse` restrictions prevent the new fold from firing when the inner `and` or inner `xor` has additional uses. The mismatched-constant tests confirmed that the `NotC == ConstantExpr::getNot(C1)` precondition prevents the fold when constants are not exact complements.

Fix Weakness Analysis: The main suspected weaknesses were poison/undef constants, vector constant matching, constant `A` interactions with existing xor-with-constant canonicalizations, one-use commuted matching, and unusual APInt widths. None produced an incorrect transformation. Some constant-A cases canonicalized to a different stable form such as `(X & ~C) ^ (A ^ C)` rather than the direct `(X | C) ^ A`, but Alive2 proved those transformations correct and differential tests found no mismatches. Scalable vectors caused Alive2 tooling limitations, but opt transformed the pattern as expected; fixed-vector Alive2 checks cover the elementwise semantics. Runtime difftesting occasionally had executor limitations/crashes for invalid pointer stores or very wide return types, but those were symmetric between original and optimized IR and not attributable to the patch. All tests in the manager were marked tested with all five Phase 1 strategies covered.
