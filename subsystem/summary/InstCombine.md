# Subsystem Knowledge for InstCombine

## Elements Frequently Missed

*   **Poison-Generating Flags (`nsw`, `nuw`, `exact`, `inbounds`, `disjoint`, `samesign`)**: These flags are frequently retained when instructions are moved, reassociated, or when their guarding conditions (like `select` instructions) are removed. The optimization pass misses that the conditions guaranteeing these flags may no longer hold in the new context, leading to incorrect poison generation.
*   **Fast-Math Flags (`nnan`, `ninf`, `nsz`)**: The pass frequently misses the semantic differences of these flags across different instruction types (e.g., `select` vs. `minnum`/`maxnum` or binary operators). It often blindly copies or propagates these flags from inner to outer operations, or from conditional to unconditional operations, without properly intersecting them.
*   **UB-Implying Attributes (`noundef`, `range`)**: When intrinsics or instructions are relaxed to return `poison` for edge cases, or when their operands are simplified, the pass frequently forgets to drop attributes that escalate `poison` to immediate Undefined Behavior (UB).
*   **NaN Sign Bits and Payloads**: The pass frequently misses the requirement to preserve the sign bit and payload of NaN values. Optimizations like folding conditional negations to `fabs` or simplifying based on demanded floating-point classes often incorrectly overwrite or clear the NaN sign bit.
*   **Undef vs. Poison Semantics**: The pass frequently conflates `undef` and `poison`. It misses that `undef` can evaluate to different values at different use sites (e.g., in masked merges or vector selects) and incorrectly upgrades `undef` to `poison`, or treats `poison` as a safe wildcard (e.g., matching a zero-broadcast mask).
*   **Pointer Provenance**: When simplifying pointer comparisons (e.g., replacing one pointer with another because they compare equal), the pass frequently misses that pointers with the same address can have different provenances, leading to invalid memory access assumptions.
*   **Multi-Use Instruction Contexts**: When performing Known Bits or Demanded Bits analysis, the pass frequently misses checking if an instruction has multiple uses. It erroneously applies context-specific constraints (valid only at one use site) to simplify the root instruction globally.
*   **Non-Power-of-Two Integer Widths**: The pass frequently assumes that bitwise masking tricks (like using `BitWidth - 1` as a mask) work universally, missing the fact that these mathematical equivalences break down for non-power-of-two integer types (e.g., `i33`).
*   **Vector Lane-Crossing Dependencies**: In vector optimizations, the pass frequently misses that lane-crossing operations (like `shufflevector`) break element-wise assumptions. It incorrectly applies scalar known-bits or demanded-elements logic to individual lanes without accounting for cross-lane data movement.

## Patterns Not Well Handled

### Pattern 1: Unconditional Execution of Conditionally Guarded Operations
The optimization pass struggles when converting conditional control flow (like `select` instructions or short-circuiting logical AND/OR) into unconditional sequences (like bitwise AND/OR, `umax`, or direct arithmetic). The core issue is that the original condition acts as a guard against poison-generating flags, UB-implying attributes, or out-of-bounds operands. When the pass folds the pattern, it frequently reuses the original instructions but fails to strip flags like `nsw`, `samesign`, or `range`. Consequently, edge-case inputs that were previously bypassed now unconditionally execute the flagged instructions, propagating `poison` or triggering UB.

### Pattern 2: Invalid Fast-Math Flag Propagation in Floating-Point Folds
High-level floating-point simplifications—such as folding a `select` into a binary operator, converting conditional negations to `fabs`, or commuting truncations with intrinsics—are not well handled regarding Fast-Math Flags (FMF). The pass often takes the union of flags or blindly copies flags from a conditionally executed instruction to an unconditionally executed one. This causes the optimized code to make overly aggressive assumptions (e.g., assuming no infinities or NaNs) on paths where those assumptions are invalid, leading to miscompilations that violate strict IEEE-754 semantics.

### Pattern 3: Context-Leakage in Multi-Use Instruction Simplification
The pass does not well handle the simplification of root instructions that have multiple uses across different basic blocks. When optimizations like Demanded Bits or Known Bits are triggered from a specific use site, the pass may utilize local dominating conditions (e.g., an `icmp` guard or `llvm.assume`). However, it incorrectly applies these local facts to modify the shared root instruction in-place. This leaks context-specific assumptions to all other uses of the instruction, corrupting the data flow for execution paths that do not share those constraints.

### Pattern 4: Unsafe Pointer Arithmetic and Comparison Canonicalization
Patterns involving the reassociation, merging, or comparison of `getelementptr` (GEP) instructions are frequently mishandled when no-wrap guarantees are absent or mixed. For example, the pass attempts to merge GEPs with mixed-sign offsets while retaining the `inbounds` flag, failing to realize that intermediate calculations might temporarily step out of bounds. Similarly, it folds pointer comparisons into direct integer/index comparisons without verifying `nuw`/`nusw` flags or accounting for `inttoptr` truncations, ignoring the fact that pointer arithmetic can wrap around the address space.

### Pattern 5: Mishandling of Poison/Undef in Vector Lane Operations
Vector transformations involving `shufflevector`, vector `select`s, and vector constants are highly prone to bugs when `poison` or `undef` elements are present. The pass often attempts to distribute operations across `select` branches or deduplicate binary operations by matching shuffle masks. However, it fails to handle the propagation of `poison` correctly—such as treating a `poison` lane as a safe `0` for deduplication, or unconditionally propagating a `poison` constant when a guarding vector `select` is removed. Additionally, it incorrectly assumes `undef` conditions in vector selects evaluate to a specific boolean value, leading to overly aggressive dead-code elimination.

### Pattern 6: Speculation and Hoisting of Non-Speculatable Instructions
The pass does not well handle the movement or operand substitution of non-speculatable instructions (like integer division, remainder, or memory loads). Patterns that attempt to sink a division after a `select`, hoist an `extractelement` above a division, or substitute a variable operand with a constant based on a conditional check frequently introduce immediate Undefined Behavior. The pass fails to recognize that the transformation exposes the non-speculatable instruction to `poison` values, out-of-bounds indices, or zero divisors that were safely masked or guarded in the original control flow.
