# Subsystem Knowledge for DivRemPairs

## Elements Frequently Missed

*   **Poison-Generating Flags (`exact`):** The optimization pass frequently overlooks the implications of flags like `exact` on division instructions (`sdiv`, `udiv`). It fails to recognize that these flags narrow the domain of defined inputs, causing the instruction to yield `poison` when conditions (like the dividend being a multiple of the divisor) are not met.
*   **Data Dependency Implications:** The pass misses the semantic shift that occurs when transforming two independent instructions (division and remainder) into a dependent chain. It fails to verify that the "parent" instruction (the division) in the new chain is as well-defined as the instruction it is helping to replace (the remainder).

## Patterns Not Well Handled

### Pattern 1: Unsafe Reuse of Constrained Instructions for General Synthesis
This pattern involves the transformation of a `rem` instruction into an arithmetic sequence (`sub(a, mul(div, b))`) by reusing an existing `div` instruction that shares the same operands. The optimization handles this poorly when the existing `div` instruction carries optimization hints or flags, specifically `exact`.
*   **Issue:** The optimization assumes the `div` result is always safe to use for calculating the remainder. However, if `div` is marked `exact`, it produces `poison` when the remainder is non-zero.
*   **Why it is not well handled:** The pass does not strip the `exact` flag from the reused division instruction, nor does it freeze the operand. Consequently, the `poison` generated by the division propagates to the synthesized remainder, rendering undefined a value that was originally well-defined.
