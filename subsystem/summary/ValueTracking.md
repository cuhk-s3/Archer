## Elements Frequently Missed

*   **Optimization Flags (`nsw`, `samesign`)**: The analysis frequently overlooks that flags like `nsw` (No Signed Wrap) or `samesign` alter the domain of defined values. Ignoring these leads to treating instructions as equivalent or safe to hoist when they actually produce `poison` on specific inputs where the unoptimized code was well-defined.
*   **Attributes Implying Immediate UB (`noundef`)**: The analysis often checks for general speculative safety (e.g., `readnone`, `nounwind`) but misses attributes like `noundef` on return values or parameters. This causes the compiler to hoist calls that trigger immediate Undefined Behavior on `poison` inputs, bypassing original control flow guards.
*   **Floating-Point Special Values (Signed Zeros, Negative NaN, Infinity)**: The distinction between `+0.0` and `-0.0`, the sign bit of NaN, and the specific arithmetic of Infinity (e.g., `0 * Inf`) are frequently missed. The compiler often treats zeros as identical or assumes standard integer comparison logic applies to FP sign bits, leading to invalid sign inferences or missed NaN checks.
*   **Scope of Fast Math Flags (FMF)**: The analysis frequently conflates flags on a controlling comparison (e.g., `fcmp nsz`) with the semantics of the instruction being controlled (e.g., `select`). It incorrectly assumes that relaxed constraints on the *condition* allow for relaxed precision in the *result*.
*   **Ordered vs. Unordered Comparison Constraints**: When analyzing `select` instructions with flags like `nnan`, the analysis often drops the "ordered" property of the underlying comparison. This leads to transformations that fail to handle NaN inputs correctly, assuming they are impossible rather than just unordered.

## Patterns Not Well Handled

### Pattern 1: Unsafe Speculation of Guarded Instructions
The optimization pass struggles with patterns where an instruction (or call) is guarded by a `select` or branch to prevent Undefined Behavior (UB) or Poison. This occurs when the instruction carries flags like `nsw` (which generates poison on overflow) or attributes like `noundef` (which triggers UB on poison). The compiler incorrectly identifies these instructions as safe to speculatively execute or fold into intrinsics (like `smax`). By hoisting the instruction out of its guard or converting the guard into an unconditional intrinsic, the compiler exposes the program to UB/Poison that was originally unreachable.

### Pattern 2: Conflation of Comparison Semantics with Selection Semantics in FP
This pattern arises when the compiler analyzes floating-point `min`/`max` idioms constructed from `fcmp` and `select`. The compiler frequently applies the semantics of the comparison to the selection. For example, if an `fcmp` has `nsz` (No Signed Zeros), the compiler assumes the `select` can also ignore signed zeros. Similarly, it may treat `+0.0` and `-0.0` as interchangeable for pattern matching because they compare equal, ignoring that `select` must preserve the exact bitwise value. This leads to transformations that return the wrong sign or incorrectly relax strict inequalities to non-strict ones.

### Pattern 3: Incomplete Analysis of Floating-Point Arithmetic Corner Cases
The compiler fails to handle complex edge cases in floating-point arithmetic logic, specifically regarding NaN generation and propagation. This is evident in `fmul` analysis, where the logic `(A != Inf) && (B != 0)` is incorrectly assumed to prove the result is not NaN, missing the `0 * Inf` case where operands are swapped. It also appears in `minnum` analysis, where the compiler applies integer-like logic to the sign bit of NaNs, incorrectly inferring that a "negative" NaN implies a negative result, despite `minnum` semantics dictating that NaNs are discarded.

### Pattern 4: Incorrect Logical Inversion with Mismatched Flags
The analysis handles logical inversions of comparisons (e.g., `ult` vs `uge`) poorly when optimization flags differ between the two instructions. Specifically, it identifies two comparisons as inverses based solely on their predicates, failing to verify that flags like `samesign` are consistent. This allows the compiler to substitute a defined boolean result from a normal comparison with a `poison` result from a flagged comparison, leading to miscompilations when the operands have differing signs.
