# Subsystem Knowledge for SimplifyIndVar
## Elements Frequently Missed

*   **Potentially Trapping Instructions**: Instructions that can trigger runtime exceptions or faults, such as integer division (`sdiv`, `udiv`) and remainder (`srem`, `urem`), are frequently missed when evaluating the safety of code motion or expression expansion.
*   **Control Flow Guards and Dominance**: The conditional branches, basic block dominance relationships, and loop-internal guards that protect unsafe operations from executing under invalid conditions (e.g., checking for a zero divisor) are ignored during the hoisting process.
*   **Speculative Execution Safety Checks**: The optimization pass misses verifying whether an instruction is safe to speculatively execute at the new insertion point (e.g., using LLVM's `isSafeToSpeculativelyExecute`). It assumes that loop-invariant expressions can always be safely evaluated in the preheader.

## Patterns Not Well Handled

### Pattern 1: Unsafe Hoisting of Guarded Trapping Instructions
When simplifying induction variable comparisons, the optimization pass often identifies loop-invariant operands and attempts to hoist them to the loop preheader to reduce loop overhead. However, it fails to handle cases where these operands are generated by potentially trapping instructions (like division or remainder) that are explicitly guarded by conditional branches within the loop body. By moving the instruction to the unconditional preheader, the pass strips away the protective control flow. This causes the trapping instruction to execute unconditionally before the loop begins, potentially triggering runtime exceptions (e.g., division by zero) that the original programmer explicitly guarded against.

### Pattern 2: SCEV Expansion Bypassing Control Dependencies
The `SimplifyIndVar` pass heavily relies on Scalar Evolution (SCEV) to analyze induction variables and expand loop-invariant expressions outside the loop. This pattern becomes problematic when the underlying operations of the SCEV expression are control-dependent on conditions inside the loop. When the SCEV expander materializes the operations in the loop preheader, it does so unconditionally. The pass does not well handle the mapping between the mathematical SCEV representation and the original control dependencies of the IR, leading to the generation of unsafe, unconditional operations that violate the safety guarantees of the unoptimized code.
