# Subsystem Knowledge for InstCombine
## Elements Frequently Missed

* **Poison-Generating Flags and Attributes (`nsw`, `nuw`, `exact`, `inbounds`, `samesign`, `disjoint`, `range`, `noundef`)**: The optimization pass frequently fails to drop these flags when an instruction's operands are modified, when it is moved out of a conditional guard, or when it is merged with other instructions. Retaining these flags in a new, less restrictive context causes valid values to incorrectly evaluate to `poison` or trigger Undefined Behavior (UB).
* **Fast-Math Flags (`nnan`, `ninf`, `nsz`)**: These flags are often incorrectly propagated from conditionally executed floating-point operations to unconditional `select` instructions, or blindly copied during the commutation of nested intrinsics. This leads to invalid assumptions about the absence of NaNs, infinities, or signed zeros, resulting in miscompilations when special values are encountered.
* **Vector Constants with `poison` or `undef` Elements**: When matching patterns or folding operations (like shuffles, bitwise NOTs, or masked merges), the pass often treats `poison` or `undef` lanes as safe wildcards. However, substituting or unconditionally evaluating these constants can propagate `poison` to lanes that were previously protected by conditional logic.
* **Non-Power-of-Two and Large Integer Types**: Transformations that rely on bitwidth-specific properties (e.g., assuming `BitWidth - 1` forms an all-ones mask, or zero-extending constants instead of sign-extending them for types larger than 64 bits) frequently fail for non-standard or very wide integer types, leading to incorrect bitwise operations or shift amounts.
* **NaN Payloads and Sign Bits**: Optimizations that fold conditional negations or sign manipulations into absolute value (`fabs`) intrinsics often miss checking for the possibility of NaN inputs. This inadvertently clears the sign bit or destroys the payload of NaNs, violating IEEE-754 semantics.
* **Pointer Provenance and Strict Aliasing Rules**: The pass sometimes treats pointers as simple integers, replacing one pointer with another solely based on an equality comparison (`icmp eq`). This misses the fact that pointers with the same address can have different provenances, leading to invalid memory accesses.
* **Multiple Uses of an Instruction (Single-Use Checks)**: Simplifications based on demanded bits or specific use-case contexts are frequently applied to instructions with multiple uses. The pass misses checking if the instruction is strictly single-use, inadvertently corrupting the value for other uses that demand all bits or different properties.

## Patterns Not Well Handled

### Pattern 1: Folding Conditional Guards into Unconditional Operations
The optimization pass frequently attempts to convert conditional constructs (like `select` instructions or short-circuiting logical AND/OR operations) into unconditional sequences of bitwise or arithmetic operations. This pattern is not well handled because it fails to account for the fact that the original condition acted as a guard against `poison` or UB. When the guard is removed, instructions that were previously safe are executed unconditionally with potentially invalid inputs (e.g., out-of-bounds shifts, poison-generating annotations), causing `poison` or UB to propagate to the final result.

### Pattern 2: Improper Flag Propagation During Instruction Commutation, Merging, or Substitution
When the compiler commutes nested operations, merges multiple instructions (such as PHI nodes or consecutive `getelementptr` instructions), or substitutes operands, it often blindly copies flags (like Fast-Math Flags or `inbounds`) from the original instructions to the newly created ones. This pattern is flawed because the new instruction may operate on a broader range of values or intermediate states that no longer satisfy the strict conditions of the original flags. The correct approach—intersecting flags or dropping them entirely—is frequently bypassed, leading to overly restrictive instructions that produce `poison`.

### Pattern 3: Hoisting or Sinking Non-Speculatable Instructions Across Control Flow or Selects
The pass struggles with safely moving non-speculatable instructions (such as integer division, remainder, or out-of-bounds vector extracts) across `select` instructions or control flow boundaries. By hoisting these operations before their guards or sinking them after a `select` that can yield a `poison` condition, the compiler evaluates them in a context where their operands might be invalid (e.g., a zero divisor). This transforms a safely guarded operation into one that introduces immediate Undefined Behavior or runtime traps.

### Pattern 4: Context-Sensitive Simplification and Demanded Bits on Multi-Use Instructions
The compiler attempts to simplify instructions by analyzing the specific bits demanded by their users or by computing known bits based on surrounding control flow (e.g., the true/false arms of a `select`). This pattern is poorly handled when the context-sensitive assumptions are incorrectly applied globally to the instruction, or when the instruction has multiple uses with different demands. Applying restricted demanded bits to a multi-use instruction corrupts its value for other users, and cross-contaminating known bits between `select` arms leads to invalid constant folding.

### Pattern 5: Transformations Altering Intermediate Value Bounds or Signs
Optimizations that algebraically rearrange expressions—such as converting left shifts to multiplications, negating `select` operands, or merging GEP offsets with mixed signs—often alter the bounds or signs of intermediate calculations. This pattern is problematic because the compiler retains no-wrap flags (`nsw`, `nuw`, `inbounds`) that were only valid for the original sequence. The transformed sequence might temporarily overflow or step out of bounds during intermediate steps, which violates the retained flags and introduces `poison`.

### Pattern 6: Pointer Equality and Cast Simplifications Ignoring Provenance and Truncation
The pass attempts to simplify pointer comparisons and memory operations by folding `inttoptr` casts or substituting pointers that evaluate as equal. This pattern is not well handled because it ignores the underlying complexities of LLVM's memory model. Folding pointer comparisons into direct integer comparisons ignores the truncation of upper bits that occurs during the cast, changing the boolean outcome. Similarly, substituting pointers based on equality strips away crucial provenance information, rendering the optimized pointers invalid for subsequent memory operations.
