### Strategy 1: fmul with NaN and negative zero

- **Target**: `fmul` instruction where both operands have known sign bits, but one or both can be NaN.
- **Rationale**: The patch infers the sign bit of `fmul` by checking if the sign bits of both operands are known and equal. However, if one operand is NaN and the other is a negative zero, the result could be NaN with a different sign bit, or if the NaN has a sign bit of 1 and the other operand has a sign bit of 1, the result might be NaN with a sign bit of 0 or 1 depending on the platform. The logic `if (*KnownLHS.SignBit == *KnownRHS.SignBit) Known.signBitMustBeZero(); else Known.signBitMustBeOne();` assumes that the sign bit of the result of `fmul` is the XOR of the sign bits of the operands. This is true for normal numbers, but for NaNs, the sign bit of the result is not guaranteed to follow this rule. If `fmul` produces a NaN, its sign bit might not be 0 even if the operands have the same sign bit. The patch does not check if the operands can be NaN before applying this logic.
- **Expected Issue**: The sign bit of the result of `fmul` might be incorrectly inferred as 0, leading to incorrect optimizations (e.g., `copysign` folding to `fabs`).

The bug is that `computeKnownFPClass` infers the sign bit of `fmul` as the XOR of the operands' sign bits, even if the result is `NaN`.
For example, `0.0 * Inf` produces `NaN`. The sign bit of this `NaN` is not guaranteed to be 0 (the XOR of the sign bits of `0.0` and `Inf`).
On some platforms (like x86), `0.0 * Inf` produces `-NaN` (sign bit 1).
However, the patch infers the sign bit as 0, which allows `InstCombine` to fold `copysign(x, fmul(0.0, Inf))` to `fabs(x)`.
This is incorrect because `copysign(x, -NaN)` should be `-fabs(x)`.
I will report this bug.
